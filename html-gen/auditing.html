Regulatory requirements refer to the laws, rules, and regulations that govern specific industries or business practices. For example, the financial services industry is subject to numerous regulations, including the Securities Exchange Act of 1934, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002, and the Dodd-Frank Wall Street Reform and Consumer Protection Act. These regulations set forth specific requirements for financial reporting, internal controls, and other areas. Auditing standards, on the other hand, are the guidelines and principles that auditors follow when conducting audits. These standards are set by professional organizations such as the American Institute of Certified Public Accountants (AICPA) and the International Auditing and Assurance Standards Board (IAASB). Auditing standards provide a framework for auditors to ensure that they conduct audits in a consistent, professional, and objective manner. The intersection of regulatory requirements and auditing standards is crucial because they both influence the conduct of auditors. Regulatory requirements set the minimum threshold for what must be included in an audit, while auditing standards provide the framework for how that audit should be conducted. For example, the Sarbanes-Oxley Act of 2002 requires that publicly traded companies have an independent audit committee, establish internal controls, and conduct annual audits. The auditing standards established by the AICPA and IAASB provide the guidance for how those audits should be conducted, including the use of specific audit techniques and the reporting of findings. Auditing standards also help ensure that audits are conducted in a consistent manner across industries and jurisdictions. For example, the International Standards on Auditing (ISAs) established by the IAASB provide a common framework for audits conducted in accordance with international auditing standards. In summary, regulatory requirements and auditing standards influence the conduct of auditors by setting the minimum threshold for what must be included in an audit and providing the framework for how that audit should be conducted. By following these requirements and standards, auditors help build trust and confidence in financial reporting and ensure that organizations are complying with applicable laws and regulations.
